Heinrich Böll

From Simple English Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Heinrich Böll (December 21, 1917 in Cologne – July 16, 1985 in Langenbroich the Eifel, south of Bonn) was a German writer and winner of the Nobel Prize for Literature in 1972.

[edit] Life

Böll was born in Cologne. His family were strong Catholics and pacifists. He went to school from 1922 to 1937. After his abitur, he started a training to become a bookseller. He didn't go to the Hitler Youth, but he had to join the German army in the World War II. In 1939 he started to study German literature in Cologne, but he had to break for his army service.

In 1945 he became prisoner of war by the Americans. The Americans wanted to educate the Germans to became a democracy. So they encouraged prisoners with talent to write. Böll and some other young writers founded the group 47.

Böll wrote a lot of social critical literature. His main themes were the repression in the way of life by conserative catholic forces and the unsolved problem of old Nazis in the society.

In the 1970's he critizised the losing of civil laws by the fight against terrorism (of the RAF, Red Army Fraction, a left-winged political terror group in the 1970's in Western Germany). For that reason the Bild-Zeitung, an aggressive, conserative yellow press newspaper, started a campaign against him.

In the late 1970's he started to look for Human Rights in the Eastern Blok, where some friends of him lives, too. He helped the Soviet dissidents Alexander Solshenizyn and Lev Kopelev.

In the 1980's he worked for the peace movement and protested against the nuclear armament in Western Germany.

He went back to the Roman-Catholic Church, which he left 1976 by protests, before he died.

[edit] Heinrich Böll Foundation

The Green Party of Germany gave its foundation the name of Böll, Heinrich-Böll-Stiftung. Aims of the foundation are international cooperation, supporting democratic thinking and civil laws and ecological thinking.
