Moai (pronounced MO-EYE) are stone statues on Easter Island. Each moai is made out of one large stone although some have an extra stone on top of the head. Most were made from the volcanic rock in the Rano Raraku area of the island. Moai are often called "heads" but most of them do have shoulders, arms, and a body, which have become buried over the years. The moai are between 2.5 and 10 metres tall and, on average, weigh 14 tonnes. There are more than 1000 moai placed all around the shoreline of the island plus nearly 400 more which were left not yet finished at Rano Raraku.

A close up of the moai at Ahu Tahai (Photo by Bjarte Sorensen)

When, why, and how they were made is still a mystery today. It is believed that they were built about 1000 years ago by Polynesians, who lived there at the time and were made to honour their ancestors and bring good luck. Making the statues would have needed a lot of time and effort and to move them, they may have used wooden sledges or rollers or even "walked" them by rocking the moai along. The youngest moai found was built around 1350 and it is believed that, around that time, there began a time of cold weather, little food, and war between the peoples. By the 1800s, all the moai had been pushed over. Today, they are slowly being restored.
